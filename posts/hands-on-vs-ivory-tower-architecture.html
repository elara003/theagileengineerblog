Ivory Tower vs. Hands-On Architects

Finding the right balance between vision and implementation

The Two Extremes

In nearly every engineering organization, there's an ongoing tension around what a software architect should actually do. On one end of the spectrum sits the "ivory tower" architect—someone who designs systems from above, producing diagrams and documents but rarely touching production code. On the other end is the "hands-on" architect—deeply embedded in the codebase, writing code alongside the team, sometimes to the point where strategic thinking takes a back seat.

Neither extreme serves teams well. The best architects I've worked with navigate somewhere in between, adjusting their approach based on the team's needs, the project phase, and organizational context.

The Ivory Tower Architect

We've all seen this archetype: the architect who spends weeks crafting elaborate system diagrams, writes lengthy architecture decision records, and presents grand visions in steering committee meetings. They speak in abstractions and patterns, referencing industry frameworks and best practices.

The problems with this approach are predictable:

  • Disconnection from reality: Designs that look elegant on a whiteboard often crumble when they meet real constraints—legacy systems, team capabilities, operational requirements
  • Loss of credibility: Teams stop trusting architectural guidance when it comes from someone who hasn't written production code in years
  • Slow feedback loops: Without hands-on implementation, architects don't learn what actually works until it's too late to course-correct
  • Over-engineering: Distance from implementation encourages adding abstraction layers that solve theoretical problems rather than real ones

The Fully Hands-On Architect

The reaction to ivory tower architecture often swings to the opposite extreme: architects who spend all their time coding, reviewing PRs, and fixing bugs alongside the team. They're in every standup, picking up tickets, and deeply familiar with every corner of the codebase.

This approach has its own failure modes:

  • Missing the forest for the trees: When you're deep in implementation details, it's hard to step back and see systemic issues or opportunities
  • Becoming a bottleneck: If the architect is the best coder on the team, they often become a dependency for critical decisions and implementations
  • Neglecting cross-team concerns: System-wide issues like observability, security patterns, and integration standards get less attention
  • Reactive rather than proactive: All energy goes into current sprint work, leaving no capacity for anticipating future needs

Finding the Balance

The most effective architects I've observed operate on a sliding scale, adjusting their involvement based on context. Here's what that looks like in practice:

Stay Close Enough to Code

You don't need to be the most prolific committer, but you should be writing code regularly. This might mean:

  • Implementing proof-of-concept work for new architectural patterns
  • Pairing with team members on complex technical challenges
  • Building internal tools or frameworks that embody architectural principles
  • Taking on a small feature end-to-end each quarter to stay grounded

Create Space for Strategic Thinking

Block time specifically for stepping back from implementation. Use this time to:

  • Review system metrics and identify emerging patterns or problems
  • Talk to teams about their pain points and friction
  • Research how other organizations solve similar challenges
  • Document decisions and their rationale for future team members

Teach Rather Than Dictate

The best architectural influence happens through enablement, not mandates. Instead of prescribing solutions, help teams understand the principles and trade-offs so they can make good decisions independently. Your goal is to multiply your impact through others.

Signals You've Gone Too Far in Either Direction

Signs of Ivory Tower Drift

  • You can't remember the last time you ran the test suite locally
  • Your architecture diagrams reference components that don't exist yet
  • Teams implement your designs differently than you expected—and you're surprised
  • Developers roll their eyes when you join technical discussions

Signs of Getting Lost in the Weeds

  • You can't articulate the system's three biggest architectural risks
  • Cross-cutting concerns keep getting deferred because everyone's focused on features
  • You're the go-to person for code reviews but haven't updated the architecture docs in months
  • Other teams are making integration decisions without your input

Adjusting to Context

The right balance shifts depending on circumstances:

  • Early-stage projects: Lean more hands-on. Architecture emerges from working code, and early decisions have outsized impact.
  • Mature systems: Shift toward strategic work. The foundations are set; focus on evolution, optimization, and cross-system concerns.
  • Crisis mode: Get hands-on. When systems are breaking, you need to be in the trenches understanding what's actually happening.
  • Scaling teams: Focus on teaching and documentation. Your job is to help others make good decisions without you.

Conclusion

The ivory tower vs. hands-on debate is a false dichotomy. Effective architecture requires both strategic vision and practical grounding. The skill is knowing when to zoom in and when to zoom out—and being honest with yourself about where you're spending your time.

If you find yourself gravitating toward one extreme, consciously schedule activities that pull you toward the other. Architecture is ultimately about making systems better, and you can't do that from either a penthouse suite or a basement server room alone.
